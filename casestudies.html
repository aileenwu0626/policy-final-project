Case Studies

Case Study 1: Edward Snowden and U.S. Intelligence

Context: In 2013, Edward Snowden, a former NSA contractor, leaked and revealed classified documents that indicated mass surveillance data programs that the U.S. National Security Agency had in secret from Congress and the public. Containing records of mass interception of phone calls and electronic activities between U.S. citizens and foreign citizens, bringing light to collections incited debate on surveillance practices and the respect for citizen privacy.

Impacts: Since the leaks, 49% of Americans approved of releasing classified information for the reason that it served public interest. However, 54% of the public also believed that the government should legally pursue criminal charges against leakers (Geiger, 2018). The overall trend, however, suggests a greater distrust in the government’s ability to protect its citizens’ data and an even greater overall belief (57% of American citizens) that the government shouldn’t be able to monitor their private communications.

Key Lessons: This case plays a big role in calling attention to the trade-offs of secrecy in the name of national security and trust. The USA Freedom Act was passed to limit how the information collected was used, storing records only for relevant national investigative uses if issued a court order (Zetter, 2015). It shows the importance of transparency policies to take form with concerns of both being fair and open to the citizens, while preserving security. Data tools can be restricted in their accessibility to organizations and groups that are determined not to have the right to free access. At the same time, being in possession of certain information can be useful and give citizens assurance that the government has more control over aspects of their safety and privacy.

Case Study 2: Flint Water Crisis

Context: The City of Flint, Michigan had changed its water supply in 2014 from Great Lakes’ Lake Huron to the Flint River in efforts to save money by relying on a cheaper water supplier. The issue with the transition was that the necessary precautions and corrosion control treatment were not taken, leading to lead poisoning through the pipage that would put the health of thousands of households at risk. Facts and figures were not clearly shared, and necessary actions were taken far too late.

Impacts: The major issue began when the water tests resulted in Flint being in violation of the Safe Drinking Water Act, and the absence of corrosion control treatment was reported to the EPA. The Michigan Department of Environmental Quality had excluded two lead level samples from their initial report, which put them in federal mandate levels (Kennedy, 2016). In the process of the city’s recovery, children have already been tested to being exposed to concerningly high levels of lead. Overall acknowledgment of the issues and lack of precaution had been downplayed, eventually leading to irreversible health effects like the outbreak of Legionnaires’ disease (University of Illinois Chicago School of Public Health, 2019).

Key Lessons: The Flint incident shows how delaying or releasing inaccurate official data can embed a long legacy of distrust in the operators and organizations responsible for the health and quality of the public and its environment and resources. Without fully disclosing initial setbacks and taking responsibility for them, related professionals and researchers will have a harder time responding in time to help with figuring out solutions to problems and implementing actions. Policies should revolve around mandatory testing/reporting protocols for hazardous environmental risks, regardless of concerns about backlash and how marginal issues may seem. All relevant findings should be requested and reported without omission of any information.
