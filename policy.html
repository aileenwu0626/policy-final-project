Policy Options and Recommendation

Policy Options Considered

Fundamentally, there should be established uniform federal standards for non-sensitive information that should be made available. Currently, different organizations and factions of the government have control over when and what they publish. This allows for the inconsistencies and the selective disclosures that give way to establishing misleading information and a lack thereof.

Part 1: Establishing Federal Data Disclosure Standards and Oversight

The first recommendation is that a federal act be past on requiring a minimum data disclosure standard for every federal agency with time-mandated updates for non-sensitive information. From a more cohesive standpoint, it promotes a default string of information that is guaranteed to the public, including concerns up for further investigation or acknowledgment with future details. An investigative office of data integrity should be established to enforce compliance and report violations, misinformation, and accountability measures to Congress and the public. Whenever information is to be taken down, replaced, or documented, responsible parties should post visible notices of the related activities and expected times for data reinstatement.

Part 2: Balancing Transparency and Security in Federal Information Access

At the same time, the nation has to find a balance between what should be released and what being released would harm the security of federal intel and the nation’s citizens. Using a council comprised of experts from multiple relevant groups on federal intelligence (ex., Department of Justice, Department of Homeland Security, academia), two to three general levels of information should be defined. Enforcement of releasing fully public data should include open datasets, budgets, environmental reports, and other general non-sensitive agency operations that citizens and other organizations can freely refer to. Other levels of more sensitive information can be placed under conditional access for researchers, journalists, and other oversight bodies through a secure conditional system. In general, secrecy should only be temporary for the right reasons. Transparency and accountability include being crystal clear about the whens and wheres of all activity related to the information in mind.

Part 3: Leveraging AI for Automated and Transparent Government Data Release

The implementation of AI/machine learning models and data tools can help default and automate the release of government datasets in an easily accessible form for the public and researchers to access. The planners and specialists can agree on the data that deserves to be published by default, thus programming AI to be able to read, clean, and format the data to automatically be accessible, lessening the interactions between the government and the process of determining whether or not to release it. In this way, it gives the government less leeway to formulate data-backed agendas and manipulate how or what fundamental data is configured and released.

Recommendation

My final recommendation incorporates all three policy options as multiple cogs of one functioning system. The federal government should establish a comprehensive Automated Open Government Data Framework that mandates minimum disclosure standards for all federal agencies while protecting legitimate national security interests through a tiered classification system enforced by an independent Office of Data Integrity. This framework would utilize AI and machine learning to automatically process, format, and publish non-sensitive government data on regular schedules. A multi-stakeholder council of intelligence, security, and civil liberties experts would define clear access levels—ranging from fully public datasets to conditionally accessible materials for credentialed researchers and journalists—with all classification decisions subject to time limits and periodic review. Any modifications to published data would require visible public notices explaining the rationale and timeline, ensuring that transparency and accountability remain the default while secrecy is permitted only when genuinely necessary and temporarily justified.
